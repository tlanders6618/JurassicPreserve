Jurassic Fun Facts: Not Every Big Reptile Was A Dinosaur

Out of the three mentioned, only stegosauruses are dinosaurs. Plesiosaurs and pterosaurs are both just large reptiles. This is primarily because dinosaurs were terrestrial (land dwelling) animals; plesiosaurs were aquatic, and pterosaurs spent a lot of their time in the skies, sometimes over the ocean. Additionally, dinosaurs have a distinctive way of standing (with their legs beneath them, similar to how dogs stand) that sets them apart from other reptiles (e.g. crocodiles have their legs at their sides). Plesiosaurs don't even have legs, and pterosaurs had their own unique way of walking. Due to the change in sea levels over time, the fossil record has some gaps in it, so it's unknown exactly how the animals evolved. However, it is known that plesiosaurs and pterosaurs evolved during the same time period as the dinosaurs, most likely diverging from the same common ancestor, and pterosaurs were most likely the reptile that was most closely related to the dinosaurs.

Technically there are two types of plesiosaurs: pilosauromorphs (which had short necks) and plesiosauromorphs (which had long necks, the ones most people think of when hearing plesiosaur); the longest plesiosauromorph neck was 22 feet/7 metres long. Both types could grow as big as dinosaurs, with larger ones being around the size of tour buses, while smaller ones were only car-sized. But contrary to popular depictions, plesiosauromorphs' necks weren't twisty and snake-like. Due to the many muscles and thick bones in their necks (required to support its length and weight), their necks were fairly stiff and remained facing straight forwards. This was also necessary for swimming, as if the neck wasn't stiff, the water's currents would've pushed against it and disrupted the reptile's balance and movement.

The Loch Ness monster. A plesiosaur. Note that the neck correctly appears stiff, not serpentine. (Plesiosaurus dolichodeirus. HETAKA, CC0 4.0).

It's unknown exactly why plesiosauromorphs had such long necks, but they were likely ambush predators and long distance swimmers, so their necks could've helped them reduce water resistance when they swam, as well as allowed them to watch prey while hiding their body. Pilosauromorphs were likely pursuit predators, and more agile than plesiosauromorphs, but both were the apex predators of the ocean. Both had similar diets, being opportunists who ate ammonites, crustaceans, mollusks, sharks, other animals' eggs, baby pterosaurs, and possibly even each other. Of course, larger plesiosaurs tended to eat larger prey.

Despite being marine animals, plesiosaurs didn't have gills, and needed to periodically resurface in order to get air. But they were still well adapted for water, having the ability to hear and smell underwater, as well as vision adapted for the dark depths. Unlike most reptiles, plesiosaurs gave birth to live babies (i.e. got pregnant), around 1-3 at a time. This is likely because dragging their large bodies onto land to lay eggs was too impractical. Plesiosaurs evolved into their well-known forms shortly before the Jurassic, and went extinct in the subsequent Cretaceous period, along with the dinosaurs.

As for pterosaurs, there are too many different types of them to mention, with pterodactyls being by far the most well known due to being the first pterosaur to be discovered; like plesiosaurs, pterosaurs evolved during the Triassic and lived until the Cretaceous extinction. With how many different types there were, they varied a lot in size; the very earliest ones were tree-dwellers the size of robins (pterodactyls were around the size of ducks), while some pterosaurs during the Cretaceous were a metre or two taller than giraffes, with wingspans of the same length. During the Jurassic, the largest pterosaur species was in-between the two extremes, with a size and wingspan comparable to an albatross (which has the largest wingspan of any extant bird). As with plesiosaurs, it's unknown how exactly pterosaurs evolved, including how they evolved to gain flight.

The Nemicolopterus crypticus, one of the earliest and also smallest pterosaurs, around the size of a robin. (Tree-dwelling pterosaur. Chuang Zhao, non commercial use).

Pterosaurs were the first non-insects to gain flight, through adaptations such as hollow bones (like birds) and skin-like wings fused with their hands (like bats). Despite this, they were perfectly capable of walking as well as flying, as unlike bats, they still had fingers that weren't fused with their wings. All pterosaurs were carnivores and used their flight to their full advantage, actively searching for their prey, but their exact diet varied based on their species and niche. Some hunted terrestrially, and preyed upon lizards, insects, eggs, baby dinosaurs, and/or dinosaur carcasses. Others searched for prey in the ocean, eating fish and mollusks and crustaceans. These pterosaurs could likely float like ducks, entering the water when they found prey, and eating it (similar to a stork or pelican) before taking off back into the skies. As a result, some species of pterosaur had big throat pouches like pelicans, and others had mouths full of needle-like teeth, though they all had elongated necks and beak-like snouts.

The Quetzalcoatlus northropi, one of the last and also largest pterosaurs, bigger than a giraffe. (Quetzalcoatlus. Johnson Mortimer, CC0 3.0).

It's completely unknown how pterosaurs flew, especially the larger ones, but despite their size, the larger pterosaurs are estimated to have reached speeds of 50-70 mph, comparable to the speed of a hummingbird. However, their reproduction is known to be similar to that of other reptiles, laying soft-shelled eggs and burying them in the dirt to keep them safe while they incubated.
